Design Map

From a Physics Outcome to an Interactive Mission Planner

This instructor begins with a course outcome and NASA/JPL resources. The Interactive Design Map makes the scientific model, learner action, validation rules, and delivery requirements explicit enough for a testable first build.

Interactive Design Map

Learning Context

Why this activity exists

Introductory astronomy or physics students often understand that planets orbit the Sun but struggle to explain why a spacecraft cannot simply launch toward Mars at any time. This short, formative activity connects orbital motion, travel time, and launch position. Students reason about timing instead of memorizing an isolated launch-window fact.

Interaction Design

What learners do

Learners predict whether Mars should be ahead of or behind Earth, then adjust its departure phase angle with an accessible slider or numeric control. A live diagram shows an idealized Hohmann transfer while Mars continues in orbit. Learners use arrival mismatch to refine the angle, receive explanation-based feedback, and explain why an opposite-Mars launch misses the target.

Expert Content

What the artifact must accurately use

Use an idealized heliocentric Hohmann model: coplanar circular orbits; Sun central body; Earth = 1 AU; Mars = 1.523679 AU; 1 AU = 149,597,870.7 km; μ = 1.32712440018 × 1011 km³/s². Calculate transfer time, speeds, delta-v values, and phase angle. Validate ≈258.9 days, 44.4° ahead, and 2.94 / 2.65 / 5.59 km/s. State all model limits.

Artifact Specification

What the finished artifact must be and do

Create a 7–10 minute Canvas formative interactive with deterministic client-side calculations. Render and update the diagram with SVG or Canvas. Provide keyboard-operable controls, a visible numeric input, high contrast, and text equivalents. No drag-and-drop, time limits, decorative animation, external APIs, or runtime dependencies. Package as Canvas-compatible SCORM 1.2; complete at the final task; include a textual results table.

In this case, “expert content” is not simply subject vocabulary. It includes the mathematical model, assumptions, constants, expected values, and limits that make the generated simulation scientifically accountable.

Test specification

The Prompt Becomes a Test Specification

The prompt does not ask AI to “make an orbital-mechanics simulation.” It specifies the learning problem, the model, the calculations, the expected results, and the conditions under which the result is valid.

Test Specification

Design Map → Test Specification

Create an accessible, browser-based Canvas-compatible SCORM 1.2 activity for introductory astronomy or calculus-based physics. Students use a simplified orbital model to explain why an Earth-to-Mars mission must launch when Mars is properly positioned relative to Earth.

Create a 7–10 minute formative interactive called Earth-to-Mars Transfer Window Explorer. Use deterministic client-side calculations and an idealized heliocentric Hohmann-transfer model; do not use an LLM, external API, or hard-coded results to calculate orbital values.

Scientific model: Treat Earth and Mars as coplanar bodies in circular heliocentric orbits, with the Sun as the central body. Use rEarth = 1 AU, rMars = 1.523679 AU, 1 AU = 149,597,870.7 km, and μ = 1.32712440018 × 1011 km³/s². State that this is an idealized instructional model, not a real mission-planning tool; it excludes eccentricity, inclination, launch from Earth’s gravity well, perturbations, mid-course corrections, and real ephemerides.

Calculations: a = (r1 + r2)/2; t = π √(a³/μ); v = √(μ/r); vtransfer = √[μ(2/r − 1/a)]; Δv1 = vtransfer at Earth − vEarth; Δv2 = vMars − vtransfer at Mars; φ = 180° − nMars × t, where nMars = √(μ/rMars³).

Validate: transfer time ≈258.9 days; Mars phase ≈44.4° ahead; Earth departure delta-v ≈2.94 km/s; Mars arrival circularization delta-v ≈2.65 km/s; total idealized heliocentric delta-v ≈5.59 km/s.

Learning flow and requirements
  • Start with a prediction: should Mars be ahead of Earth, behind Earth, or directly opposite Earth at launch?
  • Show a live, labeled two-dimensional diagram: Sun, both circular orbits, Hohmann-transfer ellipse, Earth’s launch position, and Mars’s position.
  • Provide a keyboard-accessible phase-angle slider linked to a visible numeric input.
  • Update and explain predicted arrival mismatch as phase angle changes; at the optimal angle, connect the result to Mars’s continued motion.
  • End with a transfer question: why would launching when Mars is directly opposite Earth miss Mars in this model?
  • Use SVG or Canvas; do not rely on drag-and-drop, color alone, time limits, decorative animation, or external runtime dependencies.
  • Provide an accessible textual results table with phase angle, transfer time, arrival mismatch, departure delta-v, arrival delta-v, and total delta-v.
  • Use a high-contrast, clearly labeled, responsive layout. Package as Canvas-compatible SCORM 1.2; complete after the final transfer question.
  • Deliver a self-contained SCORM package, editable source files, a concise README, and automated or runnable baseline-value checks.

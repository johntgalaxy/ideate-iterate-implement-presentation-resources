Expert Content Guide

Give AI something
trustworthy to use

Record instructor-approved material and tacit judgment. AI can organize and express this content; it should not decide what is authoritative.

01

Gather the verified material and judgment

Files are only part of expert content. Capture enough detail to distinguish evidence and authentic practice from a generic topic label.

Accurate material

  • Facts, definitions, formulas, procedures, and source excerpts
  • Exact terms and needed precision
  • Data, images, diagrams, rubrics, and assignments
  • Credits, permissions, and attribution

Learning evidence

  • What learners should notice, compare, calculate, interpret, or explain
  • Which details count as valid evidence
  • What feedback should point to
  • Useful examples, nonexamples, and edge cases

Tacit expertise

  • Preferred explanations and analogies
  • Corrections the instructor routinely makes
  • How experts notice novice errors
  • Professional practice and interpretive emphasis

Boundaries

  • Misconceptions and common errors
  • Claims needing careful framing
  • Assumptions and limits
  • What must not be invented, generalized, or oversimplified
02

Write the Expert Content category

Use this concise pattern in the Interactive Design Map and first-build brief.

Accurately use [approved facts, terms, sources, data, examples, and assets].
Learners should inspect or use [specific evidence].
Address [misconception or boundary] in the explanation or feedback.
Do not invent, replace, or extend [content that requires instructor verification].

Core principle

Expert Content is not a topic label.

It gives the artifact the accurate material, evidence, and boundaries it needs to support meaningful practice.
